Learn About Financial Living with Math!

The most common numbers appear in our finances: when shopping or saving.

Shopping

Mathematics that occurs in shopping often relates to a budget and different taxes.

To calculate the net cost, we have different scenarios with the pre-tax cost of the item as x:

  1. Our first version is when you only have one tax percentage (call it a%). You can either:
    1. Use a "direct" calculation way shown below
      2. Net cost = x * (100 - a)%
      Thinking process: We subtract a from 100 to have the percentage that remains after subracting tax,
      then we multiply by x with that resulting percentage
    2. Or use a "complementary" calculation way that may be slower, shown below
      3. Net cost = x - (x * a%)
      Thinking process: We calculate the tax, then subtract it from x
  2. Our second scenario is when you have more than one tax percentage. Suppose we have two taxes: b% and c%
    3. Note: The calculations depend on the policies in the shopping area.
    Most places have the Net cost = x - [x * (b%)] - [x * (c%)] = x (100 - b - c)%, in which you apply b and c tax to x
    Other places have the Net cost = x * (100 - b)% * (100 - c)%, in which you apply, b tax to x, then apply c tax to the result after b tax
    Note: don't confuse these two as they are totally different cases!

After, we compare the net cost and our budget.
If the net cost is less than or equal to our budget, we can afford it. Otherwise, we can't.

Saving

When you set aside money (principal amount) in your savings account for a period of time,
and if you give your financial institution permission to use your investment for loans, the institution will pay you back your principal amount (name it z) plus annual interest (name it d%).

Often, you choose to save for a number of years (name it y) that earns you your desired total sum, which can be compared to the total amount you planned to have (your total sum probably won't be spot on to your total planned amount).
We can calculate it two ways as shown below,

  1. Use a "direct" calculation way:
    2. Total sum = z * (100 + d)% * y
    Thinking process: We add a to 100 to have the percentage that is there after adding annual interest,
    then we multiply by y to calculate the percentage of the total after the years of annual interest, which is then multiplied by x to calculate the total amount.
  2. Or use a "complementary" calculation way:
    3. Total sum = z + (z * d% * y)
    Thinking process: We calculate the total annual interest rate considering the number of years and the principal amount, then add to the principal amount (z) to have the total.

After, we compare the total sum to our total planned amount.
If the total sum is greater than or equal to our planned total, we are successful. Otherwise, we should invest the money for more years.
